  • Some states have transitioned assessments 3 times in the last several years.
  • In most cases these states have also transitioned standards at least once.
  • In many cases states allow dual mode administration (P&P or Online).

The ESSA Era: Ch-Ch-Changes

  • ESSA has given states increased flexibility in creating accountability systems.
  • Most states now include student growth in their accountability systems.
  • Even those states with experience using calculating growth encounter challenges when transitioning between assessments/standards.
  • Knowing what to look for beforehand can minimize problems that can emerge.

Ch-Ch-Changes & Growth

  • Assessment/Standards transition can impact growth calculations, sometimes in significant ways.
  • Some states have transitioned assessments 3 times in the last several years.
  • In most cases these states have also transitioned standards at least once.
  • In many cases states allow dual mode administration (P&P or Online).
  • In some cases states have had issues with ceiling/floor effects on their tests.

Vertical Scales

  • Some states have transitioned from assessments without a vertical-scale to assessments with vertical-scales (e.g., many SBAC states)
  • Some states have transitioned from assessments with a vertical scale to assessments without a vertical scale.
  • Some states have never had a vertical scale.
  • As should be apparent, you don't need a vertical scale to calculate some form of student growth.

Vertical Scales

  • Despite not needing a vertical scale, some states use their vertical scale for growth calculations
  • A vertical scale allows for subtraction which is intuitive to users.
  • Depending upon how the scale is used, it can be critical that the scale be strongly equated from year-to-year.
  • If difference scores are calculated for students, consistent interpretation from year-to-year requires strong equating.

Norm-Referenced Growth

  • Most states do some form of norm-referenced growth (SGP or VAM) even if they have a vertical-scale.
  • Norm-referenced growth is very forgiving with regard to assessment transitions.
  • As long as students take the same assessment in any given year, we can norm their progress.
  • However, norms by their nature are limited to stating where one is in a larger distribution.

Criterion-Referenced Growth

  • Several states have shown interest in supplementing their norm-referenced analyses with growth-to-proficiency (criterion-referenced) growth.
  • Growth-to-standard is particularly prominent in English Language Proficiency testing.
  • Growth-to-standard calculation requires at least two years of data on the same scale.
  • To perform growth-to-standard across an assessment transition, some states put the old test scores on the new scale and move forward as though they have two years of data.
  • Transitioning to new standards doesn't impede growth-to-standard but will result in more difficult/lenient targets depending upon whether the standards are more difficult or lenient.

Value tables

  • Value-tables, which use the states prior and current performance levels to cross-tabulate student growth and award points based upon the transition observed.
  • In an of themself, a value-table is neither norm- or criterion-referenced. Similar to gain scores.
  • Use of value-tables requires the performance levels to mean the same thing from year to year.
  • Assessment or standards transition require resetting values in the value-table.

Mode Effects

  • Many states, transition to online assessments, have allowed dual mode administration.
  • Often obscured in status results, growth is a magnifying glass that shows these differences.
  • Unless explicity adjusted for, on every assessment and in every state I've seen significant mode effects -- large enough to impact growth results used for accountability.

Ceiling/Floor Effects

  • Tests with significant ceilings/floors can impact some growth analyses.

Due-Dilligence

  • Tests with significant ceilings/floors can impact some growth analyses.

Myriad Problems

  • Inconsistent and contradictory terminology.
  • Improperly used concepts/words/phrases.
  • Use of the results for high stakes accountability has only increased the confusion.
  • It doesn't have to be this hard.

Solution

  • How can we make student growth "simple" to understand?
  • We need better examples/analogies from outside of education to help users understand student achievement over time.
    • 401K Account planning and balance over time.
    • Weight loss plan and monitoring over time.

Growth Basics

  • We are governed by the rules of time:
    • There is a past.
    • There is a present.
    • There is a future.
  • Examining the past: Diagnosis
  • Examining the future: Prognosis

401K Planning

  • It's 2017 and you're 35.
  • You want to retire at 65 with $1,000,000 (in 2047 dollars).
  • You sit down with a financial adviser to map out a 401K contribution plan.
  • Assumptions: $0 at start, 7% return on investments, and 3% rate of inflation.
  • One million adjusted for inflation of 3% will be $2,427,262 in 30 years.
  • You will need to save $24,015 annually to reach that goal by age 65.

401K Year 1

  • Suppose at the end of Year 1 you saved $24,000.
  • You visit your adviser and she says you're "on track" to reach your financial goal.
  • Diagnosis (looking at the past year): OK.
  • Prognosis (looking forward): Adjust parameters and update annual contribution goals accordingly.

401K Year 2

  • Suppose at the end of Year 2 you have $35,000 and saved $15,000 in year 2.
  • You visit your adviser and she says you're "not on track" to reach your financial goal.
  • Diagnosis (looking at the past year):
    • Not so good when judged against long term goal (criterion-referenced).
    • $15,000 is still a lot more than most 36 year olds save (norm-referenced).
  • Prognosis (looking forward): Now have some catching up to do.
    • Lots of time so don't have to panic.
    • Adjust parameters and update annual contribution goals accordingly.
    • Possibly schedule a semi-annual check-up.

Associated Questions

  • All we've done is described the states of affairs tacking back and forth between diagnosis and prognosis.
    • We haven't made a prediction about whether you will reach your retirement goals.
    • We haven't evaluated the effectiveness of your financial adviser.
  • Student growth has been mostly concerned with questions analogous to whether the financial adviser is effective.
  • Education data can be used for much more than just the evaluation of schools and teachers.

Diagnostic Growth Uses

  • Diagnosis
    • Students with low growth can be flagged for possible remediation.
  • Prognosis
    • Students needing high growth to reach desirable outcomes should receive enrichment and more frequent monitoring.
